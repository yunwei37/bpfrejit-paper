\begin{abstract}

eBPF is widely adopted in modern production systems, yet its in-kernel JIT compilers remain under-optimized compared to mature runtimes like JVM and LLVM. The JIT intentionally trades performance for safety, and closing the gap inside the kernel would require tens of thousands of lines of safety-critical code. We find that even when optimizations are added, statically applying them regresses 68\% of real programs because optimization profitability is deployment-dependent. We observe that eBPF's safety is established at the bytecode level: the existing verifier can re-check any rewritten program with the same guarantees as the original load, and eBPF bytecode is structured enough to serve as an optimization IR. Our key insight is that deployment-time optimization can therefore be safely offloaded to userspace by operating at this level. We present \tool, a deployment-time compilation framework that enables both hardware specialization through kinsn and runtime specialization through map-value inlining and constant propagation. To express hardware-specific idioms beyond ordinary eBPF, \emph{kinsn} is an extensible instruction mechanism analogous to LLVM intrinsics, an extensible instruction mechanism analogous to LLVM intrinsics, with formally verified equivalence between each kinsn's eBPF expansion and its native lowering. We implement \tool with fewer than 600 lines of kernel modifications, transparently supporting existing eBPF ecosystem. Evaluation on representative workloads demonstrates that \tool achieves up to 40\% performance improvement, 50\% reduction in binary size, and low recompilation overhead. \tool provides a practical, upstream-compatible path toward deployment-time, extensible compilation frameworks.

\end{abstract}
